Number of Solutions to Equations:
- all you really have to do is simplify the equations like above, then use the following table:
	- there are no solutions if you get a nonsensical answer (like 3 = 5)
	- there is one solution if you get an answer like x = 5 (where the variable is variable, and could totally be that!)
	- there are infinite solutions if you get an answer that's always equivalent (like 13 = 13)

- video link: https://www.khanacademy.org/math/algebra/x2f8bb11595b61c86:solve-equations-inequalities/x2f8bb11595b61c86:num-solutions-linear-equations/v/number-of-solutions-to-linear-equations

Linear Equations w/ Unknown Coefficents:
- move all (answer variable) terms to one side of the equation, and move all other terms to the other side of the equation
- using the distributive property, group like terms together on both sides of the equation (if necessary)
- to isolate (answer variable), divide both sides by the coefficents of (answer variable)

- video link: https://www.khanacademy.org/math/algebra/x2f8bb11595b61c86:solve-equations-inequalities/x2f8bb11595b61c86:linear-eqns-unknown-coefficients/v/linear-equations-with-unknown-coefficients

Terms & Labels in Geometry:
- a point is a point, you can't move from it (or else you'd have a different point)

- a line segment is a straight line with two endpoints, denoted by a line over the letters

- a ray is a straight line (with only one endpoint) that extends infinitely in one direction, denoted by an arrow over the letters (PAY ATTENTION TO ORDER OF LETTERS)

- a line is a straight infinite path joining an infinite number of points in both directions, denoted by a dual arrow

- colinear points are points that are on the same line segment, ray, or line

- a midpoint is a point that is exactly halfway between two locations (which can be other points)

- a plane is an infinite two-dimensional surface (like an infinite piece of paper)

- a plane segment is a non-infinite two-dimensional surface (like a regular piece of paper ðŸ˜‚)

Rigid Transformations Intro:
- examples of rigid transformations are:
	- translations (simply moving a given point or shape)
	- rotations (turning a given point or shape relative to an arbitrary point)
	- reflections (reflecting a given point or shape across an arbitrary line)
	- any transformation where lengths AND angles are preserved

- examples of NOT rigid transformations are:
	- dilation (where shapes are scaled up or down relative to their original size, lengths aren't preserved)
	- stretching (where shapes are stretched in any way, both angles and lengths are potentially not preserved)

- video link: https://www.khanacademy.org/math/geometry/hs-geo-transformations/hs-geo-transformations-intro/v/introduction-to-transformations

Determining Reflections:
- steps to find reflection:
	- find coordinates for two different points e.g. (-4, -4) and (2, -6)
	- take x coordinates added together and y coordinates added together, and divide both by 2
	- repeat to another point on the line of reflection
